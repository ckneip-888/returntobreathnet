Why Wellness Culture Is Exhausting You (And the Case for Finite Tools)

If your meditation app gamifies a streak, it is not helping you. It is recruiting your attention. The honest tool has a beginning and an end. You open it. You breathe. You close it. The opposite of an attention economy.

BY CHRISTOPH KNEIP

Here is the trap most people do not see.

The category of tools that was supposed to calm you down has been quietly redesigned to do the opposite.

I am thinking specifically of the meditation app that asks you not to break your streak. The breathwork platform that rewards you with badges, the mindfulness service that nudges you with notifications, builds a community feed, runs a leaderboard, suggests you upgrade your subscription, sends you a recap of your practice statistics on Sundays.

I do not think the people building these things are bad people. I think they are running a business model that needs your attention to survive. Attention is what they sell to investors. Streaks generate attention. Communities generate attention. Notifications generate attention.

So the wellness product, in order to keep existing, has to do what every other product on your phone does — capture you.

The problem is that the thing you came for was the opposite of capture.

Here is what I believe.

A tool designed to calm your nervous system cannot, in good faith, also be designed to keep you on the screen.

These two design imperatives are at war with each other. Whichever one is winning is the one shaping your nervous system. If the product is engaging you, it is engaging your sympathetic system. If it is rewarding you, it is shaping a dopamine loop. If it is sending you streak notifications, it is recruiting you back into the same attention economy that broke you in the first place.

This is why so many people who have done all the work are still exhausted. They did the work inside a tool that was metabolizing them while they used it.

Here is a test.

When you close the app you use for breath or meditation — do you feel finished, or do you feel obligated to come back tomorrow?

If it is the second one, that app is not your tool. You are its tool.

Here is what is possible.

A different category of product is needed. I would call it a finite tool. It has the following characteristics:

A finite tool has a beginning and an end. You open it. You use it. You close it. It does not greet you, recap you, congratulate you, or grade you.

A finite tool does not have a streak. It does not care if you used it yesterday. Your breath does not care either.

A finite tool does not have a community feed. Your nervous system is not a social network.

A finite tool does not optimize. It does not get smarter at extracting your time. It is the same on day one as it is on day three hundred. The whole point is that the tool is dumb, and you are the one becoming wiser through use.

A finite tool is the opposite of an attention economy.

I am writing this in the spirit of full disclosure: this is the philosophy that Return, the app we are building, is committed to. No streaks. No infinite optimization. You open it. You breathe. You close it. That is the product.

I am not interested in your attention. I am interested in your nervous system.

These are not the same thing.
